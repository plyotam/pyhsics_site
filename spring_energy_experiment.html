The Mechanics of Springs & Friction

An interactive guide to understanding the physics of elastic potential energy, static forces, and energy dissipation through work done by friction.

Part I: Characterizing the Spring (Static Analysis)

The first goal is to find the spring constant (k), a measure of a spring's stiffness. We do this by applying a known force (hanging weights) and measuring the resulting stretch (elongation). The experiment relies on two key principles:

  • Newton's First Law: In a state of static equilibrium (when the weights are hanging still), the net force is zero. This means the upward pull of the spring perfectly balances the downward pull of gravity.
  • Hooke's Law: For an ideal spring, the force it exerts ($F$) is directly proportional to its elongation ($\Delta l$). The formula is $F = k\Delta l$.

By measuring the force applied by different masses and the corresponding elongation, we can create a graph to find a precise value for 'k'.

Finding 'k' with a Graph

By plotting Force vs. Elongation, the slope of the best-fit line gives us the spring constant, $k = \frac{\Delta F}{\Delta (\Delta l)}$.

Part II: Measuring Friction (Dynamic Analysis)

The Energy Transformation Story

Plotting distance vs. elongation squared reveals a linear relationship, whose slope is used to calculate $\mu_k$.

The second goal is to find the coefficient of kinetic friction ($\mu_k$) between a box and a surface. This part uses a more advanced principle:

  • Work-Energy Theorem: The net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy ($W_{net} = \Delta KE$). Since the box starts and ends at rest, its $\Delta KE$ is zero. Therefore, the total work done on it must also be zero.

This creates an energy balance: the positive work done by the spring (converting stored potential energy into motion) is exactly cancelled out by the negative work done by friction (dissipating energy as heat).

Synthesis: Connecting the Concepts

This experiment is designed in two sequential parts because the result from the first part is essential for the second. You cannot find the coefficient of friction without first knowing the stiffness of the spring you are using.
